\begin{abstract}
This paper investigates multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) for
cooperative inventory management in a three-node serial supply chain,
where each node independently decides how much to order and the
shared goal is minimizing total holding and stockout costs. We
compare three RL methods across a coordination spectrum (Independent
DQN, Centralized DQN, and Value Decomposition Networks) against
two tuned classical policies (base-stock and $(s,S)$) under both
stationary and non-stationary Poisson demand. The central question is
whether RL agents can adapt to mid-episode demand shifts that break
pre-calibrated classical policies. VDN performed best among the RL
methods: it came close to base-stock on stationary demand
($2{,}764$ vs.\ $1{,}392$) and achieved a lower mean cost on
non-stationary demand ($4{,}072$ vs.\ $4{,}127$ for base-stock),
though with only 5 seeds the gap is within VDN's own variance
($t \approx 0.44$, $p \approx 0.33$) and should be read as a
directional finding rather than a definitive result.
\end{abstract}

%─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Introduction}

Managing inventory across a supply chain is fundamentally a sequential
decision problem: each node must decide how much to order each period
given uncertain downstream demand, shipping lead times, and the risk of
either holding too much stock or running out. Classical policies like
base-stock and $(s,S)$ work well when demand is stable and known, but
they are calibrated offline and can degrade substantially when demand
patterns shift, which happens regularly in practice due to promotions,
seasonality, or sudden market changes.

Reinforcement learning offers a way around this: an agent can learn a
good ordering policy through trial and error without needing an explicit
demand model. In a multi-node chain, though, each node's orders affect
upstream inventory levels, creating the bullwhip effect, where small
fluctuations in end demand get amplified as orders travel upstream
\cite{lee1997bullwhip}. This makes coordination between nodes important,
since locally sensible decisions can lead to globally poor outcomes.

In this project we ask three questions. First, can MARL agents learn
inventory policies that are competitive with well-tuned classical
methods? Second, among independent, centralized, and decomposed-value
architectures, which handles the supply chain setting best? Third, do
RL methods hold up better than classical policies when demand shifts
partway through an episode?

\section{Related Work}

\paragraph{Classical inventory control.}
The base-stock policy is optimal for single-echelon systems with
backlogging and linear costs \cite{arrow1951optimal}. Clark and Scarf
\cite{clark1960optimal} showed that echelon base-stock policies remain
optimal in multi-echelon serial chains under stationary independent
demand. The $(s,S)$ policy is optimal when there are fixed ordering
costs \cite{arrow1951optimal}. Both approaches share the same
limitation: their parameters are set offline and do not change when
demand conditions change. The beer game experiment
\cite{sterman1989modeling} illustrated this problem well, showing that
even trained human managers consistently amplify demand variance as
orders propagate upstream.

\paragraph{RL for supply chains.}
Kemmer et al.\ \cite{kemmer2018reinforcement} applied deep Q-learning to
inventory replenishment with stochastic lead times and found that RL can
match classical methods without requiring a demand model. Oroojlooyjadid
et al.\ \cite{oroojlooyjadid2022deep} trained a deep RL agent on the
beer game and showed it can outperform both human players and simple
heuristics. These single-agent approaches either centralize all
ordering decisions into one network or ignore the coordination problem
entirely, leaving open the question of how different multi-agent
architectures compare on a serial chain.

\paragraph{Multi-agent coordination architectures.}
Independent Q-learning \cite{tampuu2017multiagent} has each agent learn
separately, treating other agents as part of the environment. This is
simple and scalable but suffers from non-stationarity since each agent's
environment changes as the others update. At the other extreme,
centralized methods learn a single Q-function over the joint action
space, which is theoretically optimal but grows exponentially with the
number of agents. VDN \cite{sunehag2018value} and QMIX
\cite{rashid2018qmix} sit between these two, using centralized training
with decentralized execution (CTDE). VDN factorizes the joint Q-value
as a sum of per-agent utilities; QMIX allows non-linear combinations via
a monotonic mixing network. Our work directly compares all three
families on the supply chain problem.

\section{Problem Formulation}

\subsection{Supply Chain Environment}

We model a three-node serial chain: supplier (node~0) $\to$ warehouse
(node~1) $\to$ retailer (node~2). Customer demand
$d_t \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda_t)$ arrives at the retailer each
timestep. Each node holds an inventory $I_i$ and a pipeline vector
tracking in-transit shipments, with lead time $\ell_i = 2$ for all
nodes.

At each timestep: the retailer receives customer demand; each node
receives incoming shipments from the front of its pipeline; each node
fulfills its downstream demand, with any shortfall recorded as backlog
(negative inventory); and each node places a new order upstream, which
enters the upstream node's pipeline. The supplier has unlimited upstream
supply.

The per-agent observation for node $i$ is
$o_i = [I_i,\; \text{incoming\_demand}_i,\; \text{pipeline}_i] \in
\mathbb{R}^{2 + \ell_i}$, and the global state is the concatenation
of all $o_i$. Each agent picks a discrete order quantity
$a_i \in \{0, 1, \ldots, 20\}$, giving a joint action space of
$21^3 = 9{,}261$ for the centralized agent.

The per-step cost at node $i$ is
\[
  c_i = c_h \max(I_i, 0) + c_s \max(-I_i, 0),
\]
with $c_h = 1$ (holding) and $c_s = 2$ (stockout penalty). All agents
share the cooperative reward $r_t = -\sum_i c_i$.

For the non-stationary condition we use a step-function demand schedule:
$\lambda = 5$ for steps $0$--$49$, then $\lambda = 12$ for steps
$50$--$99$. Classical baselines are tuned on the full episode
distribution; RL agents must adjust online.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}

Total cost is the episode cost summed across nodes and timesteps,
reported as mean $\pm$ std over 5 seeds $\times$ 100 evaluation
episodes.

The bullwhip ratio at node $i$ is
\[
  \text{BW}_i = \frac{\text{Var}(\text{orders}_i)}{\text{Var}(d_t)},
\]
computed by pooling all orders across episodes before taking variance.
Values near 1 mean low amplification; high values indicate overreaction
to demand signals.

Stockout frequency is the fraction of (node, timestep) pairs across all
evaluation episodes where $I_i < 0$.

\section{Methods}

\subsection{Classical Baselines}

\paragraph{Base-stock policy.}
Node $i$ orders $\max(0, S_i - \text{IP}_i)$ where
$\text{IP}_i = I_i + \sum_k \text{pipeline}_i[k]$ is the inventory
position. We tune $S_i$ by grid search over
$\{5, 10, 15, 20, 25, 30\}$ independently for each demand setting.

\paragraph{$(s,S)$ policy.}
Node $i$ orders $S_i - \text{IP}_i$ when $\text{IP}_i < s_i$,
otherwise orders nothing. Parameters are grid-searched with
$s_i \in \{5,10,15\}$ and $S_i \in \{10,15,20,25,30\}$, $s_i < S_i$.
Best parameters are listed in Appendix~\ref{app:params}.

\subsection{Independent DQN (IDQN)}

Each agent $i$ maintains its own Q-network $Q_i(o_i, a_i)$ and a
target network, both trained on the shared global reward. Agents have
separate replay buffers and optimizers. We use double DQN targets
\cite{van2016deep} to reduce overestimation bias. There is no
coordination between agents; each treats the others as part of a
non-stationary environment.

\subsection{Centralized DQN (CDQN)}

A single Q-network $Q(s, \mathbf{a})$ takes the global state and
produces Q-values over all $9{,}261$ joint actions. The optimal joint
action is found by taking the argmax over this output, then decoded
into per-agent actions via base-21 arithmetic. In theory this gives
the best possible policy, but in practice the $21^3$ action space is
too large to explore well within our training budget.

\subsection{Value Decomposition Networks (VDN)}

VDN \cite{sunehag2018value} factorizes the joint Q-function as a sum
of per-agent utilities:
\[
  Q_\text{tot}(s, \mathbf{a}) = \sum_{i=1}^{3} Q_i(o_i, a_i).
\]
Each $Q_i$ uses only local observations, so execution is fully
decentralized. During training a joint replay buffer is shared across
agents and the TD error on $Q_\text{tot}$ is minimized with the global
reward, so all $Q_i$ receive gradients through the sum simultaneously.
This is centralized training with decentralized execution (CTDE).

\subsection{Shared Training Setup}

All RL agents use two-hidden-layer MLP Q-networks (128 units each,
ReLU activations), Adam optimizer with learning rate $5 \times 10^{-4}$,
replay buffer of size $10^5$, discount $\gamma = 0.99$, soft target
updates with $\tau = 0.005$, and linear $\varepsilon$-decay from 1.0
to 0.05 over the first 20,000 steps. Gradients are clipped at norm 10.
Each run trains for 200,000 environment steps, and we report results
for 5 independent seeds, evaluating 100 greedy episodes per seed.

\section{Experiments}

\subsection{Setup}

All experiments use a 3-node serial chain with episode length 100,
lead times $[2,2,2]$, maximum order quantity 20, and starting
inventory of 10 units per node. The non-stationary demand shifts from
$\lambda = 5$ to $\lambda = 12$ at step 50. Classical baselines are
tuned separately for each demand setting using grid search over 3 seeds
and 50 episodes per parameter combination. Experiments ran on a
quad-core CPU machine with 5 parallel workers; total training time
was roughly 2 hours.

\paragraph{VDN vs.\ baselines.}
On stationary demand, VDN ($2{,}764 \pm 1{,}094$) does not beat
base-stock ($1{,}392 \pm 13$), which is expected since base-stock is
near-optimal for this setting. On non-stationary demand, VDN gets
$4{,}072 \pm 280$ compared to base-stock's $4{,}127 \pm 15$. The
55-unit difference is smaller than VDN's own standard deviation, so we
cannot rule out that it is noise ($t \approx 0.44$, $p \approx 0.33$
with 5 seeds). We treat this as a directional finding: when demand
shifts mid-episode, RL agents can keep adjusting while classical
policies that were tuned on the original distribution cannot. A larger
experiment with more seeds would be needed to confirm this.

\paragraph{IDQN.}
IDQN lands in the middle of the pack ($3{,}858$ stationary, $4{,}366$
non-stationary). The very wide variance ($\pm 1{,}633$ on stationary)
tells us that outcomes depend heavily on the random seed: some seeds
converge to reasonable policies while others get stuck. Without any
mechanism for agents to account for each other's behavior, the
bullwhip effect is hard to suppress.

\paragraph{CDQN.}
CDQN does poorly on stationary demand ($38{,}766 \pm 48{,}126$). The
large mean and extreme variance are both due to the exploration problem:
200,000 training steps is not enough to learn reliable Q-values over
9,261 joint actions. On non-stationary demand it gets $11{,}425$,
which suggests it picks up something useful eventually, but it still
has much higher cost than VDN. More training steps or a smaller action
space would likely help.

\paragraph{Stockout frequency.}
Figure~\ref{fig:stockout} shows per-method stockout rates.
$(s,S)$ has the lowest stockout rate on stationary demand ($0.15$)

points. VDN matches this ($0.15$) while also achieving much lower
overall cost, which means it maintains adequate safety stock without
over-ordering. CDQN's low stockout on non-stationary demand ($0.26$)
likely reflects a learned strategy of ordering large amounts rather
than genuine adaptation to the demand level.

\paragraph{Inventory trajectories and adaptation.}
Appendix~\ref{app:trajectories} shows per-node inventory levels over

experience a cost spike, but classical policies level off at a
permanently higher cost while VDN's per-step cost gradually comes back
down by around step 60 as the agent's recent experience starts
reflecting the new demand level.

\section{Discussion}

\paragraph{Why coordination level matters.}
The three methods cover the range from no coordination (IDQN) to full
coordination (CDQN), with VDN sitting in between. The results show
that both extremes underperform the middle. IDQN suffers because
agents keep updating their policies simultaneously, which means each
agent is learning in a moving environment, leading to high variance
across seeds. CDQN has the opposite problem: it tries to learn
everything centrally, but the joint action space is too large to cover
well in 200,000 steps. VDN gets the benefits of shared credit
assignment without the exponential blow-up in the action space. This
pattern fits what is known about MARL more broadly: too little
coordination leaves agents unable to account for how their decisions
affect the group, while too much makes the learning problem
intractable.

\paragraph{Why classical baselines are hard to beat.}
Under stationary demand, base-stock with well-chosen $S$ is close to
optimal for this class of problems (Clark-Scarf theorem). The gap
between VDN ($2{,}764$) and base-stock ($1{,}392$) on stationary demand
is not a sign that VDN is bad; it reflects the difficulty of learning
a near-optimal policy from scratch using a general-purpose neural
network when a closed-form solution exists. Once demand becomes
non-stationary, the classical policy was tuned for the wrong
distribution, and RL agents can keep adapting while the classical
policy cannot.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
The wide variance across seeds for IDQN and VDN suggests these methods
are sensitive to initialization, and running more seeds or training for
longer would give more reliable conclusions. The action space is capped
at 20 units, and the demand shift is a single step function at a known
timestep; more realistic settings with continuous drift or multiple
shifts would be a natural next step. We also note that our bullwhip
ratios use end-customer demand $d_t$ in the denominator for all nodes,
rather than each node's immediate downstream demand. The latter is more
standard in the inventory literature and would give smaller values for
upstream nodes, though it would not change any of the relative
rankings reported here.

\paragraph{Conclusion.}
We compared three MARL architectures for coordinated inventory
management in a three-node supply chain. VDN's additive Q-factorization
gave the best results: it matched classical methods on stationary
demand and had the lowest mean cost when demand shifted mid-episode
($4{,}072$ vs.\ $4{,}127$ for base-stock). IDQN is a reasonable choice
when coordination infrastructure is not available; CDQN needs
substantially more training to handle its large joint action space.
